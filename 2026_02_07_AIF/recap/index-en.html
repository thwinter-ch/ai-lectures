CAS AI in Finance — Lecture Recap

AI Productivity Hacks for Leaders

HWZ Zürich  |  February 7, 2026, 13:00–16:45
Lecturer: Thomas Winter  |  Programme Director: Patrick Comboeuf

Session Overview

In a 3.5-hour hands-on session, 21 finance professionals built three working AI systems from scratch: a personal Writing Profile, a structured company research workflow, and a Claude-to-Notion "Second Brain." The lecture itself was created using the same tools being taught — a deliberate meta-demonstration of AI-powered content production.

What Participants Took Home

  1. Writing Style Profile — A personal voice profile document that teaches AI how you write — not like generic boilerplate
  2. Company Research Workflow — A two-stage prompt chain (Deep Research → Strategy Synthesis) that produces board-ready briefings
  3. Second Brain — A Notion database connected to Claude for automatic knowledge capture

Core thesis: AI success is not a technology skill — it is a leadership skill. The same abilities that make someone a good leader (clear delegation, quality judgment, iterative feedback) determine who succeeds with AI.

  • Directing production, not creating content — The role shifts from doing to orchestrating
  • Infrastructure, not tools — "You're not learning tools. You're building infrastructure. The return doesn't come today — it comes every day after."

The Technology Revolution

Lecture 15 Min
Open Slides

2026 is the year every technical barrier has fallen. The new bottleneck is not what AI can do — but whether humans can articulate clearly enough to delegate.

Key Concepts

  • The 2026 Breakthrough Stack: Consumer-grade hardware, sustained attention (agents stay focused for hours), persistent memory across sessions, MCP + Skills standardize tool use, autonomous browser/file interaction
  • You are the translation layer — "The bottleneck has shifted. The question used to be: 'Can AI do this?' Now it's: 'Can YOU define clearly enough what needs to be done to delegate it?'"
  • The Mini-Me race — Who builds your AI double first? You, your competitor, or nobody? Compound effects mean early movers widen the gap monthly
  • Two-layer architecture — Translation layer (where humans interact, handle ambiguity) + Task execution layer (where agents shine, reliably execute)

Live Discussion Highlights

  • Thomas showed how Claude Code fixed a broken link in the repository during the lecture in real time — the "Lecture as Code" concept in action
  • Several participants asked about tool selection; Thomas emphasized: "The question isn't which tool — the 201 skills are tool-agnostic"
03

Writing Profile Exercise

Exercise 25 Min
View Exercise Guide

Participants created personal voice profiles using the Ruben Hassid method — a structured self-interview that extracts your "writing DNA": beliefs, constraints, style preferences, and patterns.

What It Does

  • 10 questions in the "lite interview" covering 7 categories of communication preferences
  • Generates a Markdown voice profile with banned words, signature phrases, and tonal markers
  • The profile attaches to every AI conversation so outputs sound like you — not like everyone else

Observations from the Live Session

  • Thomas noted that the exercise "sometimes turns into a psychology session — it tells you something about yourself, where you're precise and where you're not"
  • Participants found it surprisingly revealing: the framework pushes past vague answers like "I like to keep things simple" and surfaces concrete preferences and instinctive reactions
  • The exercise directly builds the first "201 skill" — context quality

AI as a Leadership Skill

Lecture 15 Min
Open Slides

The most data-rich segment. Thomas presented studies showing why 80% of people abandon AI tools after 3 weeks — and what the successful 20% do differently.

Key Data Points

80% Abandoned AI tools
after 3 weeks
+40% Higher quality
within the competence zone
-19 pp Worse performance
outside the frontier
  • Microsoft Copilot study: 300,000 employees tracked. 80% abandoned AI tools after three weeks. Not 80% of low performers — 80% of everyone.
  • BCG-Harvard study (758 consultants): Within the AI competence zone: +12% more tasks, 25% faster, 40% higher quality. Outside: 19 percentage points worse than without AI.
  • The Jagged Frontier: AI capabilities don't form a clean line. They're jagged, uneven, and often invisible. "If you don't know where the frontier lies, AI doesn't just fail to help — it actively degrades your performance."

The Jagged Frontier: AI capabilities form an irregular, unpredictable boundary — not a clean line.

Key Concepts

Centaur work: clear division of labor with clean handoffs. Cyborg work: continuous human-AI interplay.

  • Centaur work — Clear division of labor. Humans do strategy, AI does drafting. Clean handoffs. Works best for well-defined tasks.
  • Cyborg work — Continuous integration. AI starts a sentence, you finish it. Back and forth, fluid. Works for creative, exploratory work.
  • The six 201 skills: Context quality, quality judgment, task decomposition, iterative refinement, workflow integration, frontier awareness — "These are the same skills that make someone a good leader."
  • The permission gap — Your most conscientious employees drop out first if you don't explicitly create permission to learn, temporarily fail, and experiment.

Live Discussion — Change Management (Extended Q&A)

This segment sparked the longest discussion of the event. Key threads:

"Let the enthusiasts take the others by the hand — but tell them it requires empathy. Not everyone gets it on the third try. Think of it like teaching a child to walk."

  • On organizational reality: "If you introduce AI without change management, the result is chaos, not collaboration. 20% will become experts, 20% will coast along, and the majority will say: 'This is nonsense.'"
  • On the performance dip: "Your best auditor is suddenly no longer the best for three months. If you don't give them permission for that dip, they'll either quit AI or quit their job."
  • On employer boundaries: "You don't have unlimited responsibility to carry along everyone who refuses. At some point, if someone can't deliver results by any path, that's also a reality."
05

Company Research Exercise

Exercise 30 Min
View Exercise Guide

A two-stage AI research workflow that produces board-ready company briefings.

Stage 1 — Deep Research (Perplexity Pro)

  • Structured research prompt that generates 30,000+ words of analysis
  • Executive summary, product table, competitor matrix, risk flags with citations
  • Participants researched their own company, clients, or competitors

Stage 2 — Strategy Synthesis (Claude)

  • Feeds Stage 1 output into framework synthesis
  • Produces: Golden Circle (Why/How/What), Business Model Canvas (9 blocks), Value Proposition Canvas (Jobs/Pains/Gains)
  • Output: 10,000-word strategy document with visual aids

Observations from the Live Session

"If you walk into a first meeting with a complete research brief plus Golden Circle plus BMC plus VPC, you're instantly the hero — because the client feels you genuinely care about their business."

  • One participant's research pulled 7,800 sources — demonstrating both the power and the challenge of "how do you find the signal in the noise?"
  • Discussion about using these as living documents: run monthly, save as Markdown, let AI identify trend shifts quarterly

Information Overload + Cognitive Limits

Key Concepts

Miller's Law: Working memory holds roughly 7 items (±2). Even CEOs and geniuses are subject to this constraint.

  • Miller's 7±2 Law — Working memory holds roughly 7 items. "Every executive in this room operates in the same 7±2 prison. So do Mark Zuckerberg and Einstein."
  • The Israeli judges study — Parole approval rates dropped from 65% after breakfast to nearly 0% before lunch. "Every decision you make in the afternoon is potentially impaired. Not by information asymmetry — by blood sugar."
  • Information escape velocity — "The leadership advantage used to be access to information. Now it's the escape velocity from information."
  • Pull instead of push — The old model (subscribe to everything, read broadly) broke down when information became infinite. The new model: define the question, let AI do the heavy lifting.
  • AI as cognitive augmentation — Not replacement. The same evolution as writing extending memory, calculators extending arithmetic, search engines extending retrieval.

Insights from the Live Delivery

  • Thomas traced the data explosion: from kilobytes (pre-1500) to petabytes (1980s), exabytes (2000s), and zettabytes (today)
  • Link to the previous exercise: "You just produced 80,000 words of research. Who reads that? That is precisely the information management problem."
  • Honest admission: "I told a board seminar in December that personal information management is the one problem I haven't solved. I tried Evernote, OneNote, Capacities — same results, different logo."
07

Second Brain Exercise

Exercise 25 Min
View Exercise Guide

Participants connected Claude to Notion and built a knowledge capture system.

What They Built

  1. Claude-to-Notion connection (one-click MCP integration)
  2. A Notion database with structured fields (topic, tags, source, action items)
  3. Auto-push instructions: say "save this" at the end of any Claude conversation and it gets summarized + written to the database

Why This Matters

"Most knowledge management systems die from friction. This system eliminates the friction."

"If you feed it regularly, patterns emerge. The AI can tell you what topics have been on your mind — it becomes a mirror of your own thinking."

Second Brain architecture: inputs flow through AI classification into a structured Notion database for retrieval.

Challenges and Workarounds from the Live Session

  • Some participants couldn't access the microphone in browser-based Claude — the mobile app worked as a fallback
  • ChatGPT users discovered it can read from Notion but not write (an OpenAI product decision, not a technical limitation)
  • Thomas helped several participants with connector setup — "These connectors can be stubborn sometimes"
  • Discussion about Wispr Flow as an alternative input method for voice capture

The Telegram Demo

Thomas showed his own advanced setup: a Telegram channel where voice messages, photos, and text are automatically classified by a serverless function and written to a tagged Notion database. "This is one small step beyond what you just built. I just narrowed the interface and added automatic classification."

08

Outlook & Reality Check

Lecture Demo 20 Min
Open Slides

The closing segment combined live demos of advanced AI capabilities with important warnings about security and sovereignty.

Part A — Live Demos

1. Lecture as Code — Showed how a participant's bug report was fixed with Claude Code in real time: "The participant said a link was broken. I told Claude to look into it. Within 2 minutes the public repository was updated and the connection repaired."

2. Hugo (OpenClaw Agent) — Demonstration of an autonomous AI agent that:

  • Manages its own LinkedIn channel
  • Generated a social media post about the lecture in real time
  • Used the Gemini API to create a custom image
  • Can be scheduled for automated posting via Typefully
  • Has been paper-trading crypto on an Alpaca API account for 2 days

3. Personalized Meeting Summaries — Showed an n8n workflow that:

  • Takes meeting transcripts + participant profiles + areas of interest
  • Produces individualized summaries for each participant
  • "Each person gets their own infographic and personalized key takeaways"

Part B — Reality Check

4. Data Sovereignty & the Cloud Act:

  • "The Cloud Act is not the reason not to go to the cloud. It stands for 'Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data' — 95% of requests are rejected by cloud providers."
  • For criminal threats: major cloud providers offer better security than most mid-sized companies can build themselves
  • For state-level threats: different calculus. FISA 702, National Security Letters operate without public disclosure
  • "For 90% of use cases, the major providers are fine. For regulated industries with competitive IP that could clash with a powerful American company — there you need full sovereignty."
  • Practical Swiss context: FINMA has approved most cloud usage for banks. Data residency requirements exist but are manageable. Private banks are typically stricter by choice, not regulation.

"Treat AI chats like emails to Jeffrey Epstein. Don't plan crimes with ChatGPT — chat logs can be subpoenaed."

6. Password Hygiene:

  • Unique password per application (auto-generated)
  • Password manager (1Password, NordPass, or Dashlane — not browser-based)
  • Two-factor authentication everywhere
  • API keys: separate per application, rotate on leak
  • Real-world case: the Colonial Pipeline hack started with a single default password (test123)

Q&A and Discussion Highlights

The session was highly interactive throughout. Key discussion threads:

Change Management and AI Adoption

  • The enthusiasm gap: Intrinsically motivated people find AI playful and see benefits. Those who feel it's imposed look for flaws and reasons not to use it.
  • Reverse mentoring recipe: Pair enthusiasts with skeptics, but demand empathy from the young enthusiasts — "You need to treat it like teaching a child to walk, not like debugging code"
  • The 80% reality: Like every technology adoption — Teams, Zoom, all had the same adoption curve. "No change management budget" is the first thing that gets cut, and then people wonder why adoption stalls.

Tool Selection and Platform Decisions

  • Claude Pro for Notion write access (ChatGPT can only read)
  • Perplexity Pro for web research with citations
  • Gemini for image generation and YouTube video interpretation
  • Claude Code for anything requiring local machine access
  • "I try to stay platform-agnostic where possible. Each tool has its sweet spot."

The "Next Level" Conversation

  • Several participants asked about building autonomous agents (OpenClaw)
  • Thomas' honest assessment: "The user experience isn't mature enough for a business audience yet. But it's coming."
  • He only started building his own VPS and Docker container two weeks before the lecture — "I'm an engineer, but I hadn't coded in 20 years. Claude Code taught me."

Recommended Next Steps

  1. This week: Use your Writing Profile in 3 different AI conversations. Notice where it's thin and refine it.
  2. This month: Run the company research exercise for a real client or competitor before your next important meeting.
  3. Ongoing: Feed your Second Brain daily. After a week, ask Claude: "What topics have been on my mind?"
  4. For the ambitious: Explore Claude Code (terminal-based) for tasks requiring local machine access.
  5. For teams: Consider the "reverse mentoring" approach — pair AI enthusiasts with skeptics, give both permission to learn.

What I Wish I'd Known

Practical tips from the live delivery:

1

The Notion connector is fragile — Plan extra time for setup. Some participants needed 10+ minutes just for the MCP authorization flow.

2

ChatGPT can't write to Notion — Only Claude and Perplexity have native write connectors. This surprised several ChatGPT Plus subscribers.

3

Prompt output length matters — 30,000-word research outputs can overwhelm some tools. Start with 5,000-word summaries for quick results; go deep only when needed.

4

Voice input varies by platform — Claude in the browser sometimes lacks microphone access. The mobile app works. Wispr Flow is a workaround for desktop voice input.

5

Gamma slides need full screen — Thomas spent part of the lecture figuring out how Gamma presents in full screen. Download as PDF or use the native presentation mode.

6

The real value is in stage two — Deep Research is impressive but overwhelming. The Strategy Synthesis (Golden Circle + BMC + VPC) makes it actionable.

7

Run monthly, evaluate quarterly — Save research outputs as Markdown files. Run them monthly. Let AI identify trend shifts quarterly. The compound effect is enormous.
